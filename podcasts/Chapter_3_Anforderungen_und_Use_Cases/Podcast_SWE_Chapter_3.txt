# Slide 123

Let me walk you through the fascinating world of **requirements** in software engineering, where we transform abstract ideas into concrete specifications that guide our development journey. Today, we're diving deep into how we capture, analyze, and validate what our software must actually do—because as we all know, getting requirements right is the difference between building a system that delights users and one that becomes a maintenance nightmare.

At the heart of requirements engineering lies the **evaluierungsphase**, our initial exploration where we transform vague stakeholder wishes into something tangible. Think of this as the architectural blueprint phase, where we ask fundamental questions: What problem are we solving? Who will use this system? What are the constraints? This phase isn't about writing perfect specifications—it's about creating a shared understanding that reduces costly misunderstandings later. We might create a **vorversion** or **prototyp** here, a simple but functional mockup that lets stakeholders see what they're getting before we've invested months of development. This early validation prevents us from building the wrong thing, which is far more valuable than building the right thing wrong.

Now, let's examine how we structure these requirements. The **use_case** technique serves as our bridge between users and developers, capturing interactions from the actor's perspective. A well-written use case isn't just a feature list—it's a narrative that describes how someone accomplishes a goal through our system. For example, when documenting how a bank customer withdraws money, we don't just say "the system processes withdrawals." Instead, we detail the entire flow: the customer selects withdrawal, enters amount, the system verifies balance, dispenses cash, and updates records. We include alternative paths too—what happens when the balance is insufficient? This level of detail transforms requirements from abstract wishes into executable specifications.

But requirements aren't just about what the system does—they're about understanding the underlying domain. This is where the **analysemodell** comes into play, creating a conceptual map of our problem space. We identify key entities like customers, accounts, and transactions, and map their relationships. This model becomes our shared vocabulary, ensuring developers and stakeholders speak the same language. It's not about implementation details yet—that comes later. Instead, it's about capturing the essence of what we're building.

Here's where things get particularly interesting. The requirements we capture aren't static—they evolve as we learn more. This is why **iterative entwicklung** shines in requirements engineering. Rather than trying to capture everything upfront, we work in cycles: we gather initial requirements, build a prototype, get feedback, refine our understanding, and repeat. Each iteration delivers a working increment that stakeholders can actually use, while our requirements become progressively more accurate. This approach works especially well when stakeholders aren't sure exactly what they want, or when the problem domain is complex.

The **product_backlog** serves as our living requirements repository in agile development. Unlike traditional specifications that become outdated, the backlog evolves continuously. It contains all known requirements, prioritized by business value and risk. Through **backlog grooming**, we regularly refine these items—splitting large features into smaller ones, adding details, and re-prioritizing based on new insights. This living document keeps everyone aligned on what's most important.

Of course, requirements engineering isn't just about capturing what we want—it's about making smart technical decisions too. This is where **lose kopplung** becomes crucial. When we design our system, we want modules that can change independently without causing ripple effects. By depending on abstractions rather than concrete implementations, we create flexibility. For instance, if our payment processing system depends on an interface rather than a specific payment provider, we can swap providers without rewriting the entire system. This principle guides our architectural decisions from the requirements phase onward.

The **dependency beziehung** in UML modeling helps us visualize these connections. When we see a dashed arrow between classes, it's telling us that one component temporarily uses another without permanent commitment. This visual language helps us spot potential coupling issues early—if too many classes depend on a single component, we might have a design problem brewing.

As we wrap up, remember that requirements engineering isn't a one-time activity at the start of a project. It's an ongoing conversation between developers, stakeholders, and the evolving system itself. The best requirements documents aren't static—they're living artifacts that grow and change as we learn. Whether we're using **prototyping** to validate assumptions, **iterative entwicklung** to refine our understanding, or **lose kopplung** to design flexible architectures, the key is maintaining this continuous dialogue. Because in software engineering, the most successful systems aren't those with perfect initial requirements—they're those where requirements and development co-evolve toward excellence.

This isn’t just a methodology; it’s a mindset that transforms how we build software, making it more resilient and responsive to change.

Let’s begin with the *Evaluierungsphase* (evaluation phase), often the first step in any project. This isn’t merely about ticking boxes or drafting documents—it’s about laying the groundwork for everything that follows. During this phase, we assess requirements, evaluate risks, and validate the feasibility of our approach. Think of it as the reconnaissance mission before the main campaign: we need to understand the terrain, identify potential pitfalls, and ensure our strategy is sound. This phase is where we ask critical questions: *What exactly are we building? Who will use it? What are the constraints?* By answering these early, we avoid costly missteps later. This is where ideas take shape in code or mockups, giving stakeholders a tangible glimpse into what the final product might look like. A [[Prototyp]] isn’t a polished, fully functional system—it’s a sketch, a sandbox where we test assumptions and refine requirements. That’s what a [[Prototyp]] does for software. It reduces risk by validating technical choices and user expectations early. For example, if we’re developing a payment processing system, a [[Prototyp]] might focus on the checkout flow, allowing users to interact with it and provide feedback. This feedback loop is invaluable—it turns abstract ideas into concrete insights, ensuring that the final product aligns with real-world needs.

But how do we organize all this work? Enter the [[Iterative_Entwicklung|iterative development]] model, a cornerstone of modern software engineering. Instead of attempting to build the entire system in one go—a recipe for disaster—we break the project into smaller, manageable chunks called [[Iteration|iterations]] or [[Sprint|sprints]]. This approach isn’t just about delivering features; it’s about continuous learning and adaptation. Every [[Sprint]] ends with a review, where we assess what we’ve built, gather feedback, and adjust our plans for the next cycle. It’s like climbing a mountain in stages: after each ascent, you pause to check your bearings, ensure you’re on the right path, and plan the next leg of the journey. This iterative rhythm keeps the project agile, allowing us to respond to changes without derailing the entire effort.

Think of it as the project’s to-do list, but far more sophisticated. The [[Product_Backlog]] isn’t static—it’s continuously refined through [[Backlog_Grooming]], where the [[Product_Owner]] and the development team collaborate to clarify priorities, break down large tasks into smaller ones, and ensure everything is well-defined and actionable. This isn’t just administrative busywork; it’s the mechanism that keeps the project focused and aligned with stakeholder needs. For instance, if a new regulatory requirement emerges, it’s added to the backlog, prioritized, and then addressed in a future [[Sprint]]. This flexibility is what makes iterative development so powerful—it embraces change rather than resisting it.

Now, let’s address a critical principle that underpins all of this: [[Lose_Kopplung|loose coupling]]. This isn’t just a technical term—it’s a philosophy that shapes how we design systems. Loose coupling means minimizing dependencies between components, allowing them to operate independently while still contributing to the whole. Imagine a team of musicians playing in an orchestra: each musician plays their part, but the magic happens when they come together harmoniously. Similarly, in software, loose coupling ensures that changes in one part of the system don’t ripple unpredictably through the rest. We achieve this through [[Dependency_Injection]], where dependencies are provided externally rather than hardcoded, or by using [[Schnittstelle|interfaces]] to abstract functionality. For example, a payment service might rely on a [[PaymentProcessor]] interface, allowing us to swap out credit card processing for PayPal without rewriting the entire system. This principle isn’t just about flexibility—it’s about resilience, making the system easier to maintain, test, and scale.

Let’s tie this all together with a real-world analogy. Picture a construction site where a skyscraper is being built. The [[Evaluierungsphase]] is like the blueprint phase, where architects and engineers assess the site, calculate loads, and plan the foundation. The [[Vorversion]] is akin to building a scale model or a mock-up of the lobby, allowing stakeholders to visualize the space before committing to the full structure. The [[Iterative_Entwicklung|iterative development]] model is like constructing the building floor by floor, with each floor representing a [[Sprint]]—after each floor is completed, the team reviews progress, addresses issues, and plans the next phase. The [[Product_Backlog]] is the project manager’s clipboard, listing every task from installing elevators to painting the walls, constantly updated and reprioritized as the project evolves. And [[Lose_Kopplung|loose coupling]]? It’s the modular design of the building itself, where each floor, wall, and utility system operates independently but contributes to the whole.

So, what’s the takeaway from all this? The journey of a software project isn’t a straight line—it’s a series of cycles, each building on the last, each informed by feedback and adaptation. The [[Evaluierungsphase]] sets the stage, the [[Vorversion]] validates the vision, [[Iterative_Entwicklung|iterative development]] delivers incremental progress, the [[Product_Backlog]] keeps the team aligned, and [[Lose_Kopplung|loose coupling]] ensures the system remains robust and adaptable. Together, these elements transform the daunting task of building software into a manageable, iterative, and ultimately rewarding process. And remember, every great software system begins with a single step—an iteration, a prototype, a well-defined requirement. So let’s embrace the process, learn from each cycle, and build something extraordinary.

Let me walk you through the critical concept of iteration duration in agile development, where timing isn't just a detail—it's the heartbeat of your entire project. Think of iteration duration like the tempo in a musical composition. Too fast, and the musicians can't keep up—they're constantly changing sheets while missing the melody. Too slow, and the audience loses interest before the crescendo arrives. In software development, a two-week iteration is like a tight allegro: perfect for quick feedback loops and rapid adaptation, but challenging when you need to implement substantial features. On the other hand, a two-month iteration resembles a grand adagio—allowing for deep architectural exploration and complex feature development, but risky when requirements evolve or when early feedback could prevent costly mistakes.

The core tension here stems from the fundamental trade-off between delivery speed and development depth. When iterations are too short, teams struggle to deliver anything beyond trivial functionality. You might complete a user story, but the system architecture remains a patchwork quilt rather than a well-engineered structure. This leads to what we call [[Starre_Abhängigkeit|rigid dependencies]]—where components become so tightly coupled that future changes trigger cascading failures throughout the system. Conversely, when iterations stretch beyond two months, the team risks falling into the complexity trap. What seemed like a straightforward feature during planning might reveal itself as a labyrinth of interconnected components, where each change requires reworking half the codebase.

Consider the architectural challenge of implementing a new payment processing system. In a two-week iteration, you might only get to implement basic credit card functionality, leaving payment gateway integrations and fraud detection for future cycles. But this incremental approach allows you to test the architectural foundation early—does your [[Dependency_Beziehung|dependency relationship]] between the payment service and database handle the load? Can you easily swap out the credit card processor for PayPal without rewriting half your system? The short iteration forces you to design for flexibility from day one, implementing [[Lose_Kopplung|loose coupling]] patterns like interfaces and dependency injection to keep components interchangeable.

Now imagine the same project with six-month iterations. You dive deep into the architecture, building elaborate data models and complex transaction flows. But when stakeholders finally see the result after months of development, they realize the payment processing system doesn't handle international currencies or doesn't integrate with their existing accounting software. The cost of retrofitting these requirements into a monolithic architecture becomes prohibitive, leading to [[Technische_Schuld|technical debt]] that compounds with each subsequent iteration.

The factors that threaten iteration success reveal why this timing sweet spot exists. New technology adoption, for instance, requires careful pacing. If you try to implement blockchain payments in a two-week sprint, you'll likely end up with a brittle prototype that doesn't handle edge cases. But if you wait for a six-month iteration to explore blockchain's potential, you might discover too late that the technology doesn't scale for your transaction volume. The [[Evaluierungsphase|evaluation phase]] becomes crucial here—building a [[Vorversion|prototype]] or [[Prototyping|prototyping]] the new technology in a controlled environment before committing it to production code.

Team dynamics also play a crucial role in determining optimal iteration length. When expert developers are spread thin across multiple projects, coordination overhead increases exponentially. A two-week iteration might feel rushed when your senior architect is only available 20% of the time. Conversely, a six-month iteration gives teams more breathing room to coordinate complex dependencies, but risks creating knowledge silos where only one person understands critical components. The [[Iterative_Entwicklung|iterative development]] approach helps here by breaking work into manageable chunks that can be assigned based on team member availability and expertise.

Perhaps the most insidious threat comes from underestimating complexity, especially in early project phases. When you're implementing your first [[Use_Case|use case]] for user authentication, you might assume it's a simple database lookup. But as you dig deeper, you realize you need to handle password resets, multi-factor authentication, session management, and integration with third-party identity providers. A two-week iteration forces you to confront these complexities early, while a six-month iteration might let you build an elegant but ultimately fragile authentication system that collapses under real-world usage.

The [[Product_Backlog]] and [[Backlog_Grooming]] processes become essential tools for maintaining iteration discipline. A well-groomed backlog ensures that each iteration contains work that's appropriately sized—not too small to create overhead, not too large to risk incomplete delivery. The [[Sprint]] planning meeting then becomes the orchestra conductor, ensuring that each team member understands their role in delivering the iteration's [[Inkrement]] without overcommitting to complexity that can't be resolved within the timebox.

Ultimately, iteration duration isn't just about calendar time—it's about creating the right conditions for learning and adaptation. Each iteration should deliver not just working software, but also insights about your architecture, your team dynamics, and your stakeholders' evolving needs. Requirements bridge the gap between the problem domain and the solution domain. They answer critical questions: What is the system supposed to accomplish? Who will use it? What are its boundaries? And how do we ensure it meets the necessary quality standards?

Let’s begin with the *tasks* that requirements define. For example, if we’re building a membership management system for a sports club, one task might be to allow an administrator to register new members. But this isn’t enough. Next, we identify the *users*—the actors who interact with the system. These aren’t just end-users; they include administrators, external systems, or even automated processes. Determining who uses the system is crucial because each actor has different needs and expectations. Finally, we must define the *limits*—the boundaries between what is inside the system and what lies outside. This includes identifying external dependencies, such as third-party APIs or hardware components, and clarifying what the system does not control. For example, if our membership system relies on a postal service to send physical membership cards, that postal service is outside our system’s control. Defining these limits helps in designing robust interfaces and managing risks associated with external factors.

It is the act of saying, "This is part of our solution, and that is not." Boundaries prevent scope creep, reduce complexity, and ensure that we focus our efforts where they matter most. For our sports club system, the boundary might include the member database and contribution calculation engine, but exclude external payment gateways or third-party email services. Defining boundaries is not just about technical decisions; it is about understanding the scope of responsibility and the interfaces through which the system interacts with the world.

To bring this to life, let’s consider how these concepts manifest in iterative project management, a topic we explored in our previous sessions. In an iterative approach, we break the project into manageable cycles—iterations—each with a fixed duration and a defined scope. Within each iteration, we continuously refine our understanding of requirements. For example, in the first iteration, we might focus on core functionality like adding members and basic contribution tracking. But as we progress, we refine our understanding of performance requirements—perhaps realizing that the system must handle thousands of members efficiently. This iterative refinement is only possible when we have a solid foundation of requirements to build upon.

Let’s take a concrete example from our Zettelkasten: the *Task Board*. This is a visual tool used in agile development to track work items across different stages—"To Do," "In Progress," "To Verify," and "Done." Each task represents a slice of functionality or a requirement that must be fulfilled. The Task Board is not just a management tool; it is a living artifact that reflects our evolving understanding of requirements. As we move tasks from one column to another, we are, in essence, validating and refining our initial assumptions about what the system must do and how it must behave.

Now, consider the *Burndown Chart*, another powerful artifact in iterative project management. This chart plots the remaining work against time, providing a visual snapshot of progress. If the actual work deviates significantly from the planned work, it signals a need to revisit our requirements. Perhaps we underestimated the complexity of a feature, or maybe we missed a critical non-functional requirement. The Burndown Chart is a feedback mechanism that ensures our requirements are not static but evolve in response to reality.

For instance, the Use Case "Add Member" outlines the steps the administrator takes and the system’s responses. This model helps us clarify system boundaries by defining who interacts with the system and how. It also serves as a basis for testing, ensuring that the system meets the requirements as understood by its users.

But how do we ensure that our requirements are accurate and complete? This is where the *Anforderungsanalyse* (requirements analysis) comes into play. This is the systematic process of capturing, structuring, and evaluating requirements. It involves interviews with stakeholders, workshops to clarify needs, and prototyping to validate assumptions. Let’s also consider the *Objektmodell* (object model), which maps the real-world entities—such as members, contributions, and addresses—into the software system. This model helps us understand the relationships and responsibilities within the system. For instance, a member object might contain methods to calculate contributions or update addresses. The object model is not just a technical artifact; it is a reflection of our understanding of the problem domain. It ensures that the system’s structure aligns with the real-world processes it is meant to support.

Now, let’s address a common pitfall: the confusion between functional and non-functional requirements. Functional requirements describe what the system does, such as "The system must allow members to log in." Non-functional requirements describe how well the system does it, such as "The login process must complete within one second." Both are essential, but they serve different purposes. Functional requirements define the system’s capabilities, while non-functional requirements define its quality. Ignoring either can lead to a system that works but is unusable, or one that is fast but insecure.

To ensure that our requirements are measurable and verifiable, we use *Metriken* (metrics). For example, we might specify that the system must handle 1,000 concurrent users without performance degradation. Or, the average time to resolve a support ticket must be less than four hours. Metrics provide objective criteria for evaluating whether the system meets its requirements. They are the yardsticks by which we judge success.

Finally, let’s talk about testing. Requirements are not just documents; they are the basis for our entire testing strategy. We start with *Modultests* (unit tests), which verify individual components in isolation. For example, we might test the method that calculates contributions to ensure it returns the correct amount. Next, we move to *Integrationstests* (integration tests), which check how components work together. For instance, we might test the interaction between the member database and the contribution calculation engine. Then, we conduct *Systemtests* (system tests) to verify that the entire system meets its requirements. Finally, we perform *Akzeptanztests* (acceptance tests) with the actual users to ensure the system fulfills their needs.

In summary, slide 142 is a reminder that requirements are not a bureaucratic hurdle but the very essence of software development. They are the lens through which we understand the problem, the compass that guides our design decisions, and the yardstick by which we measure success. Without clear requirements, development is a shot in the dark. With them, we illuminate the path forward, ensuring that every line of code we write serves a purpose and every feature we deliver adds value. So, as we move forward in this course, remember: understanding requirements is not just the first step in software development—it is the foundation upon which all other steps are built.

The key insight here is that these categories aren't arbitrary labels; they directly impact how we plan and prioritize our development efforts. When we organize requirements into logical clusters like "member management" or "communication with associations," we suddenly have a map that shows us which features belong together and how they interact. For instance, the requirement to change a member's address naturally belongs to the member management cluster, while sending messages to the association forms its own distinct group. Ladies and gentlemen, let me take you on a journey to the very heart of what makes software systems truly valuable—not just what they do, but *how well* they do it. Today, we’re exploring **non-functional requirements**, the silent guardians of quality that define the soul of your system long before a single line of code is written. These are the constraints, the expectations, the unspoken rules that transform a functional piece of software into something reliable, secure, and delightful to use. Think of them as the architectural blueprint of excellence—without them, even the most feature-rich system risks collapsing under the weight of poor performance, security vulnerabilities, or usability nightmares.

At their core, non-functional requirements answer a deceptively simple question: *How good is what we’re building?* They don’t describe *what* the system does—that’s the domain of [[funktionale_Anforderung|functional requirements]]—but rather *how* it performs those functions. Will it respond to user requests in under two seconds? Can it handle a thousand simultaneous logins without breaking a sweat? Is it so intuitive that even a first-time user can navigate it without frustration? These are the concerns that non-functional requirements address, and they are every bit as critical as the features themselves. In fact, they often determine whether a system is adopted or abandoned by its users.

Let’s ground this in a real-world analogy. Imagine you’re constructing a bridge. The functional requirements might specify that the bridge must span a river and support vehicular traffic—that’s the *what*. But the non-functional requirements? They dictate that the bridge must withstand earthquakes, resist corrosion over decades, and provide a smooth ride for drivers. A bridge that collapses under heavy traffic or rusts away in five years, no matter how beautifully designed, is a failure. Similarly, a software system that crashes under load or leaks sensitive data is doomed, regardless of how many features it boasts. Non-functional requirements are the engineering standards that ensure your system doesn’t just *work*—it *endures*.

Now, let’s dissect this concept further. Non-functional requirements are often categorized into several key dimensions, each addressing a different facet of quality. **Performance** requirements, for instance, might stipulate that 95% of system requests must be processed within two seconds under peak load. **Security** requirements could mandate that all data transmissions be encrypted using AES-256, or that user authentication must occur within a strict time window to prevent brute-force attacks. **Scalability** requirements ensure the system can grow seamlessly as user demand increases, while **usability** requirements guarantee that the interface is intuitive enough for users to accomplish their tasks without extensive training. **Reliability** requirements, such as a mean time between failures (MTBF) of at least 30 days, ensure the system remains operational when it matters most. And **maintainability** requirements, like clear documentation and modular design, make it easier for future developers to update or fix the system without introducing new bugs.

These requirements are not just abstract ideals—they must be *measurable*. This is where [[Metrik|metrics]] come into play. A non-functional requirement without a metric is like a ship without a compass: you’ll never know if you’re on course. For example, a requirement stating that the system must be "fast" is meaningless unless you define what "fast" means in concrete terms. Is it response time? Throughput? Latency? And how will you measure it? Will you use average response time, or the 95th percentile to account for outliers? Will you test under simulated load, or in a production-like environment? The answers to these questions transform vague aspirations into actionable goals. In our earlier bridge analogy, metrics would be the stress tests, load calculations, and material inspections that ensure the bridge meets its safety standards. Without them, you’re building blindfolded.

But here’s the catch: non-functional requirements are not static. They evolve as the system grows and as user expectations shift. A system that performs adequately today may struggle under tomorrow’s user load. A security protocol that’s state-of-the-art today could be obsolete in a year. This is why iterative approaches like [[Iteratives_Projektmanagement|iterative project management]] are so crucial. By breaking development into manageable cycles, or [[Iteration|iterations]], teams can continuously refine and adapt non-functional requirements based on real-world feedback. Tools like [[Burndown_Chart|burndown charts]] and [[Task_Board|task boards]] help visualize progress and identify bottlenecks early, ensuring that quality isn’t an afterthought but a constant focus. Imagine building that bridge in segments, testing each part for durability before moving to the next. That’s the power of iteration—it turns the abstract into the tangible, one step at a time.

Let’s also address a common misconception: non-functional requirements are not the sole responsibility of developers or testers. They require collaboration across the entire project team, from stakeholders who define the vision to architects who design the system’s backbone, to quality assurance teams who validate its performance. For instance, a stakeholder might insist on a high level of security, but it’s the architect’s job to design a system that can enforce encryption without sacrificing usability. Similarly, a tester might use [[White_Box_Test|white-box testing]] to probe the internal logic of a module for performance bottlenecks, while a [[Black_Box_Test|black-box test]] ensures the system meets user expectations from the outside. This interplay between different roles and techniques is what transforms non-functional requirements from a checklist into a living, breathing part of the system’s DNA.

To bring this to life, consider the example of a membership management system for a sports club. A functional requirement might state that the system must allow administrators to add new members. But the non-functional requirements? They might include that the system must handle 500 simultaneous logins during peak registration periods, that member data must be encrypted both at rest and in transit, and that the interface must be accessible to users with disabilities. Without these constraints, the system might function—but it could fail spectacularly when 500 parents try to register their kids for soccer at the same time, or it might expose sensitive member information to attackers. These are not optional extras; they are the difference between a system that *works* and one that *matters*.

In conclusion, non-functional requirements are the unsung heroes of software development. They are the difference between a system that merely exists and one that thrives. They ensure that your software is not just a tool, but a trustworthy, efficient, and enjoyable extension of your users’ lives. So the next time you’re defining requirements for a project, ask yourself: *What does “good” look like?* Because in the end, it’s not enough to build something that works—you must build something that *endures*. And that, my friends, is the art and science of non-functional requirements.

Then there's MTBF, the mean time between failures, which gives us a sense of how long we can expect the system to run smoothly before something breaks down. Imagine a restaurant where the kitchen equipment fails on average every 30 days—that's an MTBF of 30 days. A higher MTBF means greater reliability, which is crucial for systems where downtime isn't an option.

Scalability addresses how well our system grows with demand. Skalierungszeit refers to how quickly we can add resources when traffic spikes—like adding more tables and staff during a sudden influx of dinner reservations. This metric becomes particularly important for systems expecting seasonal traffic or rapid user growth. Then there's maintainability, which measures how easily we can fix problems when they do occur. The average time to repair tells us how quickly our team can diagnose and resolve issues, minimizing disruption to users.

These metrics don't exist in isolation—they're interconnected in fascinating ways. For instance, improving response time often requires architectural changes that might impact scalability. A system that scales beautifully might introduce complexity that makes maintenance more challenging. This is why we need to balance these metrics carefully, understanding that optimizing one might come at the cost of another.

Let me illustrate this with an example from our software engineering course. Imagine we're developing a membership management system for a sports club. Our performance metrics might include how quickly the system can generate a membership list export—perhaps measured in seconds. Reliability would track how often this export function fails when processing hundreds of members simultaneously. Scalability would consider how the system handles sudden spikes during registration season, and maintainability would measure how long it takes our development team to fix bugs in the export functionality.

The key insight here is that these metrics aren't just numbers we track—they represent fundamental qualities that determine whether our software system will succeed or fail in production. A system with excellent performance but poor reliability will frustrate users when it crashes unexpectedly. A highly reliable system that can't scale will collapse under its own success. This is why we must consider all these metrics holistically throughout our development process, from initial design through deployment and maintenance.

As we move forward in our lecture series, we'll see how these metrics connect to broader concepts like [[Iterative_Projectmanagement]] and [[Anforderungsanalyse]]. The metrics we define today will become the measurable targets we strive for in our [[Use_Case_Modell]] and [[Objektmodell]], guiding our architectural decisions and implementation strategies. They'll inform our [[Testebene]] approach, from [[Modultest]] through [[Systemtest]], ensuring that every layer of our system meets the quality standards we've established.

So when you're designing your next software system, remember these metrics aren't just academic—they're the difference between a system that works and one that truly delivers value to its users.

Ah, welcome back to our exploration of software engineering fundamentals! This distinction isn’t just academic—it fundamentally shapes how we design, implement, and validate our systems. So let’s dive right in.

At first glance, the answer might seem obvious, but the reality is nuanced. Technical requirements, which include specifications for programming languages, platforms, hardware, frameworks, and more, are inherently non-functional. Why? Because they don’t describe *what* the system does—they describe *how* it does it. For example, if a requirement states that the system must run on a Linux server with Java 17 and use the Spring Boot framework, it’s not defining a feature like "users can log in" or "the system calculates membership fees." Instead, it’s setting the stage for *how* those features will be implemented. Non-functional requirements are all about constraints, qualities, and operational characteristics that the system must adhere to, even if they’re not directly visible to the end user.

To solidify this, let’s recall the core definitions from our [[Anforderungsanalyse|requirements engineering]] discussions. Functional requirements answer the question, "What should the system do?" They describe the behaviors, features, and capabilities that users interact with directly. Think of them as the "verbs" of your system—the actions it performs. Non-functional requirements, on the other hand, are the "adverbs." They describe *how well* those actions are performed. They answer questions like, "How fast should the system respond?" or "How secure must it be?" Technical requirements fall squarely into this latter category because they impose constraints on the implementation without defining the implementation itself.

Let’s take a concrete example to illustrate this. Suppose we’re developing a membership management system for a sports club. A functional requirement might be: "The system shall allow administrators to export a list of all members as a CSV file." This is clearly a functional requirement because it describes a specific capability the system must provide. Now, consider a technical requirement that states: "The system shall use PostgreSQL as its database management system and shall be deployed on an AWS EC2 instance running Ubuntu 22.04." This requirement doesn’t describe any user-facing feature. Instead, it constrains the technology stack and deployment environment, ensuring consistency, scalability, and maintainability. These are non-functional requirements because they’re about the *how*, not the *what*.

This distinction is crucial for several reasons. First, it helps us prioritize and manage requirements effectively. Functional requirements are typically tied to user stories and business goals, while non-functional requirements often cut across the entire system and can have significant architectural implications. For instance, a requirement for "99.9% uptime" might necessitate redundant servers, load balancers, and failover mechanisms—decisions that ripple through the entire system design. Second, it clarifies the scope of testing. Functional requirements are validated through [[Use_Case_Modell|use cases]] and [[Testebene|test levels]] like [[Modultest|unit tests]], [[Integrationstest|integration tests]], and [[Systemtest|system tests]]. Non-functional requirements, however, require specialized testing approaches, such as [[Performance_Test|performance testing]] for response times or [[Security_Test|security testing]] for vulnerabilities. Finally, it ensures that stakeholders and developers are aligned on expectations. Users care about features, but they also care about reliability, security, and performance—even if they don’t articulate those concerns in functional terms.

Now, let’s address a common misconception: the idea that technical requirements are somehow subjective. In reality, while the *selection* of technologies (e.g., Java vs. Python, AWS vs. Azure) might involve trade-offs and preferences, the *requirement* itself is objective. Once a technical requirement is set—such as "the system must use HTTPS for all communications"—it becomes a measurable constraint that can be verified through tools like [[Metrik|metrics]] or automated checks. For example, we might define a metric that 100% of API endpoints must enforce HTTPS, and we can validate this using tools like SSL Labs or custom scripts. This objectivity is what separates non-functional requirements from purely subjective concerns, such as "the system should be easy to use," which would require user feedback and usability testing to evaluate.

To further cement this idea, let’s revisit the [[Funktionale_Anforderung|functional vs. non-functional requirements]] zettel from our knowledge base. It emphasizes that functional requirements define *what* the system does, while non-functional requirements define *how well* it does those things. Technical requirements, by their very nature, are about the *how*—the constraints and qualities that shape the implementation. They’re the guardrails that ensure our system is not only functional but also robust, scalable, and maintainable.

So, why does this matter for your projects? Because understanding this distinction helps you avoid common pitfalls. For example, if you conflate technical requirements with functional ones, you might end up over-specifying the system’s architecture in a way that limits flexibility. Imagine a scenario where a requirement mandates a specific database technology before the functional requirements are fully understood. Later, you might discover that the chosen database doesn’t support a critical feature, leading to costly rework. By keeping technical requirements as non-functional constraints, you leave room for architectural decisions to evolve based on the system’s needs rather than premature commitments.

In summary, technical requirements are non-functional because they describe the *how* of implementation rather than the *what* of functionality. They’re the constraints and qualities that shape the system’s architecture, deployment, and operation. Recognizing this distinction is essential for writing clear requirements, designing effective tests, and delivering systems that meet both functional and non-functional expectations. As we move forward in our course, keep this principle in mind—it will serve as a foundation for everything from [[Architekturentwurf|architecture design]] to [[Qualitätssicherung|quality assurance]]. And remember, in software engineering, the devil is often in the details, but the details are what make the difference between a system that works and a system that works *well*.

Let me walk you through the fascinating world of **Use Cases**—a cornerstone of modern software engineering that bridges the gap between human needs and technical implementation.

At its heart, a **[[Use_Case]]** represents a single, meaningful interaction between an external entity—what we call an **[[Akteur]]**—and your software system, aimed at achieving a specific goal from the user's perspective. Think of it as a conversation where the actor says, "Here's what I need your system to do for me," and the system responds by performing a series of coordinated actions. Whether that actor is a human user, another software system, or even a hardware device, the **[[Use_Case]]** captures the essence of that interaction in a structured way. What makes **[[Use_Case|Use Cases]]** so powerful is their ability to transform abstract user needs into concrete, testable scenarios. They don’t just describe *what* the system should do—they show *how* it will be used in real-world situations. This clarity is invaluable during the **[[Anforderungsanalyse|requirements analysis]]** phase, where stakeholders often have different—and sometimes conflicting—ideas about what the software should accomplish. By formalizing these interactions, **[[Use_Case|Use Cases]]** ensure that everyone involved—from end users to developers—shares a common understanding of the system’s purpose and behavior.

Let’s break down the anatomy of a **[[Use_Case]]** to see how it works in practice. Every **[[Use_Case]]** begins with a clear **goal**—something the actor wants to achieve. For example, in a membership management system, a **[[Vereinsmanager]]** might want to **[[Mitglieder_verwalten|manage members]]**, which includes adding, editing, or deleting member records. To make this **[[Use_Case]]** meaningful, we define **preconditions**—the state the system must be in before the interaction begins. In this case, the **[[Vereinsmanager]]** must be authenticated and authorized to perform these actions. Then comes the **main scenario**, a step-by-step sequence of actions that unfolds when everything goes according to plan. The manager selects a member, updates their details, and the system saves the changes. But real-world interactions aren’t always perfect, so we also include **alternative scenarios**—branches in the flow that handle exceptions or edge cases. What if the manager tries to delete a member who still has outstanding fees? The system would respond with an error message or prompt for confirmation. Finally, we define **postconditions**, the state the system should be in after the **[[Use_Case]]** completes. Here, the member’s data would be updated, and if applicable, notifications might be sent to relevant parties.

Now, you might wonder: How do we visualize these **[[Use_Case|Use Cases]]** to make them easier to grasp? This is where **[[Use-Case-Diagramm|Use Case Diagrams]]** come into play. These diagrams, part of the **[[UML]]** standard, provide a high-level overview of the system’s functionality by mapping out actors, **[[Use_Case|Use Cases]]**, and the relationships between them. Imagine a rectangle representing the system boundary—everything inside is what we’re building, and everything outside interacts with it. Within this boundary, we place ovals for each **[[Use_Case]]**, and stick figures or icons for the actors. Lines connect actors to the **[[Use_Case|Use Cases]]** they participate in, showing who does what. But diagrams can get even more sophisticated with relationships like **<<include>>** and **<<extend>>**. The **<<include>>** relationship is used when one **[[Use_Case]]** *must* include another as part of its normal flow. For instance, the **[[Use_Case]]** "Manage Members" might *include* the **[[Use_Case]]** "Authenticate User" because every action requires the manager to be logged in first. This avoids repeating the authentication steps across multiple **[[Use_Case|Use Cases]]** and keeps the model clean and maintainable. On the other hand, the **<<extend>>** relationship is used for optional or conditional behavior. Suppose we want to add a feature where the system automatically archives data when a member hasn’t been active for five years. This wouldn’t be part of the core "Manage Members" flow, but it could *extend* the base **[[Use_Case]]** under specific conditions. The beauty of these relationships is that they allow us to model complex behaviors without cluttering the main scenario.

Let’s consider a real-world analogy to solidify this concept. Picture a restaurant. The **[[Use_Case]]** "Order Food" represents the interaction between a customer (the actor) and the restaurant (the system). The main scenario might involve the customer selecting a dish, the waiter taking the order, the kitchen preparing the food, and the waiter delivering it. Preconditions would include the restaurant being open and the customer being seated. Alternative scenarios could include the kitchen running out of an ingredient or the customer requesting a substitution. Postconditions would be the customer enjoying their meal and the restaurant updating its inventory. Now, think about the **<<include>>** relationship: every time a customer orders food, the waiter must first greet them—that’s an included **[[Use_Case]]**. And for the **<<extend>>** relationship, imagine the restaurant offers a loyalty program where regular customers get a free dessert. This feature extends the base "Order Food" **[[Use_Case]]** but isn’t mandatory for every order. By modeling the restaurant’s operations this way, we can see how **[[Use_Case|Use Cases]]** and their relationships mirror real-world interactions, making them intuitive for both technical and non-technical stakeholders.

But why go through all this effort? The answer lies in the tangible benefits **[[Use_Case|Use Cases]]** bring to a project. First, they improve communication. By describing functionality from the actor’s perspective, they make requirements accessible to everyone involved, reducing misunderstandings and misaligned expectations. Second, they serve as a blueprint for testing. Each **[[Use_Case]]** can be translated directly into test cases, ensuring that the system behaves as intended under various conditions. Third, they guide the design and implementation phases. Developers can use **[[Use_Case|Use Cases]]** to identify the components and services needed to support each interaction, leading to a more modular and maintainable architecture. And finally, they help manage complexity. By breaking down the system into discrete, goal-oriented interactions, **[[Use_Case|Use Cases]]** prevent developers from getting lost in the details and keep the project focused on delivering real value to users.

Of course, **[[Use_Case|Use Cases]]** aren’t a silver bullet. They work best when requirements are relatively stable and well-understood, as their structured nature can be less flexible than approaches like **[[User_Story|User Stories]]** in agile environments. They also require careful scoping—defining the system boundary is crucial, as including too much or too little can lead to confusion. But when used appropriately, **[[Use_Case|Use Cases]]** transform the often chaotic process of software development into a disciplined, user-centered endeavor. They remind us that at the end of the day, software isn’t just about lines of code or architectural diagrams—it’s about solving real problems for real people. And that’s a goal worth pursuing with clarity and precision.

The use cases themselves are represented as ovals within the boundary, each one connected to the actors that initiate or participate in it. This visual representation makes it easy to see the relationships between actors and use cases, as well as the overall structure of the system.

But a use-case diagram is not just about drawing lines and shapes. It is about capturing the **relationships** between use cases, and this is where the concepts of **include** and **extend** relationships come into play. An include relationship indicates that one use case is a mandatory part of another. For example, the use case "manage members" might include the use case "authenticate user," because authentication is a necessary step before any member-related actions can be performed. This relationship helps us avoid redundancy and ensures that common functionalities are reused across multiple use cases. On the other hand, an extend relationship describes how one use case can optionally enhance another under specific conditions. For instance, the use case "manage members" might be extended by the use case "archive old member data," which is only triggered when a member has been inactive for a certain period. This allows us to model optional or conditional behaviors without cluttering the main use case with infrequent or specialized scenarios.

At this stage, it is crucial to remember that every requirement—whether functional or non-functional—must be traceable back to at least one use case. This traceability ensures that nothing is overlooked and that every feature or constraint is justified by a clear business or user need. Functional requirements describe what the system must do, such as "the system shall allow users to reset their passwords," while non-functional requirements define how well the system must perform these tasks, such as "the password reset process must complete within two seconds." By linking each requirement to a use case, we create a cohesive narrative that connects the high-level goals of the system with the detailed specifications that will drive its implementation.

Let me illustrate this with a concrete example. Suppose we are designing a system for a university library. One of our high-level use cases is "borrow a book." This use case involves several actors, including the library patron and the librarian, and it encompasses functional requirements like checking out a book, verifying the patron’s eligibility, and updating the library’s inventory. It also includes non-functional requirements, such as ensuring that the checkout process is secure and that the system can handle peak loads during the start of a semester. By modeling this use case and its relationships, we can clearly see how each requirement fits into the larger picture, ensuring that our system is both functional and reliable.

In conclusion, the process of requirements analysis is not just about gathering needs—it is about transforming those needs into a structured, prioritized, and traceable blueprint for development. By starting with high-level use cases, evaluating their importance, modeling their relationships, and ensuring traceability, we lay the groundwork for a system that is not only technically sound but also aligned with the goals and expectations of its stakeholders. This is the essence of Step 1 in our analysis, and it is a step that cannot be skipped or rushed. The clarity and precision we achieve here will echo through every subsequent phase of the project, from design to implementation to testing. So, let us embrace this process with diligence and care, for it is the foundation upon which great systems are built.

Let me walk you through the relationships between use cases in software engineering, a concept that often causes confusion but becomes crystal clear once we understand its purpose and structure.

This demonstrates how <<include>> and <<extend>> serve different purposes: inclusion for mandatory, reusable components and extension for conditional, optional behaviors.

The practical implications of understanding these relationships are profound for software development. When we properly model use case relationships, we create a foundation that guides our entire development process from analysis through design and testing. The <<include>> relationship helps us identify reusable components that can be implemented once and used across multiple features, while the <<extend>> relationship allows us to handle special cases without complicating our core functionality. So, what exactly is a **Split**? In the context of an Activity Diagram, a Split is a control node that takes a single incoming flow and fans it out into two or more parallel outgoing flows. Think of it as a traffic roundabout where one road splits into several directions. Each outgoing path represents an independent activity that can proceed *concurrently* with the others. For instance, when a customer places an online order, the system might simultaneously validate the payment, update inventory, and send a confirmation email. Each of these actions can occur in parallel, and the Split node is what initiates this concurrency. Importantly, the Split doesn’t impose any order on these parallel paths—they are truly independent, and their execution order is not predetermined.

Now, consider the counterpart: the **Join**. This is where the parallel paths converge back into a single flow. The Join node waits for *all* incoming parallel activities to complete before allowing the process to continue. It acts like a checkpoint or a synchronization barrier. Returning to our order example, the system must wait for the payment to be processed, the inventory to be updated, and the confirmation email to be sent before it can finalize the order and notify the customer. Only when all three parallel tasks are done does the Join node allow the process to proceed to the next step—perhaps logging the transaction or updating the customer’s order history. This ensures correctness and data consistency in concurrent workflows.

Let’s ground this in a more intuitive analogy. Imagine a kitchen preparing a multi-course meal. The chef (our system) receives an order (the incoming flow). At the **Split** point, the chef divides the work: one sous-chef chops vegetables, another marinates the meat, and a third preheats the oven. These tasks happen simultaneously—parallel paths in our diagram. But once all are ready, they converge at the **Join** point. Only when the vegetables are chopped, the meat is marinated, and the oven is hot does the chef proceed to cook the main course. The Join ensures nothing is missed and the meal is served correctly.

In UML notation, these nodes are represented as thick bars—horizontal or vertical—distinct from other control nodes like decision points or initial nodes. The Split bar has one incoming arrow and multiple outgoing arrows, while the Join bar has multiple incoming arrows and one outgoing arrow. This visual clarity makes it easy to distinguish parallelism from branching logic, which is crucial for avoiding confusion in complex diagrams.

It’s worth emphasizing what Split and Join *are not*. They are not decision points—they don’t evaluate conditions or choose paths based on data. That’s the role of **decision nodes** or **merge nodes**. Split and Join are purely about *parallelism* and *synchronization*. They don’t carry data; they control the flow of control. Also, they are not suitable for modeling optional behavior or alternative paths—that’s where conditional branches come into play.

The real power of Split and Join emerges when modeling systems that require high throughput or real-time responsiveness. Consider a web server handling multiple client requests at once. Each request might trigger parallel validation, logging, and response generation. Using Split and Join, we can clearly depict how these operations interleave without getting lost in tangled control flows. This modularity not only improves readability but also aligns closely with how modern systems are architected—using asynchronous, event-driven, and concurrent paradigms.

To solidify this understanding, let’s revisit the code-like structure from our Zettelkasten, but now in spoken form. Imagine a sequence starting with an initial node labeled "Start." From there, a single flow reaches a thick horizontal bar—the Split node. Two outgoing arrows lead to two actions: "Validate input" and "Log request." These actions occur independently. After both complete, their flows converge at another thick bar—the Join node. Only then does the process continue to "Process data." This structure ensures that input validation and logging are both finished before moving forward, maintaining data integrity and system reliability.

In conclusion, **Split and Join** are the unsung heroes of Activity Diagrams. They enable us to model the inherent parallelism of real-world systems with precision and clarity. By dividing work into independent streams and synchronizing them at the right moment, they help us design robust, efficient, and scalable processes. Whether you're modeling a banking transaction, a manufacturing workflow, or a user interaction in a mobile app, mastering Split and Join will elevate your ability to capture the true dynamics of your system. So next time you see a thick bar in an Activity Diagram, remember: it’s not just a line—it’s the gateway to concurrency and the backbone of parallel execution.

At its core, the analysis object model represents the **problem domain** as it exists in reality, not as we might want to implement it in software. Think of it as creating a detailed map of the territory before building the city on top of it. This model focuses exclusively on identifying the fundamental **objects**, their **attributes**, and the **relationships** between them—without yet considering methods, technical specifications, or implementation constraints. The beauty of this approach lies in its ability to reduce complexity by isolating the essential concepts that matter most to the stakeholders.

Now, let's examine how we actually construct such a model. The process begins with two complementary strategies that work together like a detective's magnifying glass and notebook. First, we use a **checklist of categories** to systematically identify potential objects. These categories include tangible **things** like a "book" or "car," **roles** such as "librarian" or "driver," **processes** like "checkout" or "driving," **events** such as "accident" or "graduation," **organizations** like "university" or "company," and many others. This structured approach ensures we don't miss critical elements that might otherwise remain hidden in the problem space.

The second strategy involves **textual analysis**—scanning through use case descriptions and requirements documents to locate all **nouns**. Each noun becomes a potential candidate for an object or concept in our model. However, we must exercise caution here, as not every noun represents a meaningful object. For instance, while "time" might appear as a noun, it's typically better modeled as an attribute of an event rather than a standalone object. The key insight is that we should combine both approaches: using the checklist to validate our noun candidates and ensuring we capture all relevant concepts, even if they don't explicitly appear as nouns in the documentation.

Let me illustrate this with a concrete example from a university system. Imagine we're analyzing a use case about students enrolling in courses. The nouns we might extract include "student," "course," "enrollment," "department," and "semester." Using our checklist, we recognize that "student" represents a role, "course" is a thing, "enrollment" is a process, "department" is an organization, and "semester" is an event. Each of these becomes a candidate for our analysis object model. We then need to determine which of these should be modeled as full objects versus simple attributes. For instance, while "semester" might initially seem like a date attribute, it actually represents an important temporal concept that affects many processes, so it deserves its own object status.

This brings us to a critical distinction that often trips up beginners: the difference between **concepts** and **attributes**. A common mistake is modeling what should be a separate concept as merely an attribute of another object. Consider the example of a "club membership" system. One might be tempted to model "status" as a simple attribute of a "member" object, perhaps as a string like "active" or "inactive." However, this approach loses valuable domain knowledge and creates unnecessary complexity later. The "status" isn't just a label—it represents a complex concept with its own lifecycle, rules, and relationships. It might need to track when the status changed, who approved it, and what actions are permitted under each status. Therefore, "status" should be modeled as its own object with relationships to both the member and the club, capturing the full richness of the domain.

The analysis object model serves multiple crucial purposes throughout the software development lifecycle. First, it provides a **shared understanding** between developers, domain experts, and stakeholders. When we model the problem domain accurately, everyone gains a common vocabulary and mental model of how the system should work. Second, it helps **reduce complexity** by breaking down the problem into manageable, well-defined components. Third, it serves as the **foundation** for subsequent design and implementation phases, ensuring we build the right system rather than just building the system right.

Let's contrast this with what the analysis object model is *not*. It's not a class diagram for implementation, as it doesn't include methods or technical details. It's not a data model for database design, as it doesn't concern itself with storage structures or optimization. And it's not a solution model, as it focuses exclusively on the problem domain without considering technological constraints. This purity of purpose makes the analysis object model uniquely valuable—it captures the essence of what needs to be built before we decide how to build it.

To solidify this understanding, consider the university example again. A proper analysis object model would include objects like Student, Course, Enrollment, Department, and Semester, with clear relationships between them. The Student object would have attributes like name and student ID, but not methods for enrollment logic. The Enrollment object would represent the process of a student signing up for a course, with its own attributes like enrollment date and grade. This model captures the essential structure of the university domain without prematurely constraining how we might implement these concepts in code.

In conclusion, mastering the analysis object model transforms how you approach software development. Instead of jumping straight to technical solutions, you learn to first understand and model the problem domain in all its richness. This approach leads to more robust, maintainable systems that truly serve their intended purpose. The analysis object model isn't just a technical artifact—it's a way of thinking that separates good developers from great ones. When you can look at a problem domain and see not just the surface-level nouns but the underlying concepts and their relationships, you've mastered the first and most important step in building exceptional software systems.

Let me walk you through the key concepts on this slide, which introduces the fundamental building blocks for modeling alternative, optional, and iterative flows in activity diagrams. These constructs—represented by the fragments `[cond]`, `[else]`, `[cond]` again, `opt`, and `loop(min,max)`—are not just syntactic sugar but powerful tools that allow us to capture the dynamic behavior of systems with precision and clarity.

For example, in a process for handling customer orders, a `[cond]` fragment could check whether the item is in stock. If it is, the flow proceeds to "Ship order"; if not, the `[else]` fragment directs us to "Notify customer of delay."

The `opt` fragment, on the other hand, introduces the concept of *optionality*. It represents a block of activities that may or may not be executed, depending on a condition. This is particularly useful for modeling scenarios where certain steps are contingent on external factors or user choices. Imagine a workflow for submitting a research paper where the `opt` fragment could encapsulate the optional step of including supplementary materials—something that authors can choose to add or omit. The beauty of `opt` lies in its ability to keep the diagram clean while still conveying the flexibility inherent in the process.

This is where the [[Pin_(UML)|Pins]] in our [[Activity-Diagramm]] become crucial—they help us model the data flow into and out of these operations, ensuring we don't lose sight of the information that drives our system's behavior.

Let's return to our club management example to see how this plays out in practice. When a member wants to leave a department, our system needs to perform several operations: verify the member's current department, update the department's member list, notify the department head, and possibly log the action. The [[Swimlane]] concept helps us visualize who's responsible for each operation—perhaps the member initiates the process, the system handles the verification, and the department head receives the notification. When we model operations, we're not just writing code—we're capturing the essence of how the real world works. This is the essence of good software engineering—transforming abstract requirements into concrete, well-designed operations that bring our systems to life.

Let me walk you through the essential concepts of early and later iterations in requirements engineering, as we see them on this slide. When we first start capturing requirements, our primary focus is on establishing the boundaries of our system and identifying who or what interacts with it. This isn't just about drawing boxes on a diagram—it's about fundamentally understanding what belongs inside our system and what exists in the external world.

The first step is defining the [[Systemgrenze|system boundary]], which acts like a fence around our software. Everything inside this fence is our responsibility to build and maintain, while everything outside interacts with our system but isn't part of it. This boundary isn't arbitrary—it's carefully drawn based on what functionality we need to implement versus what already exists in the real world. For example, in an online shopping system, the product catalog and payment processing would typically be inside our system boundary, while the credit card payment service from a bank would exist outside it, as we're just using their functionality rather than building it ourselves.

Once we've established this boundary, we identify our [[Akteur|actors]]—the entities that interact with our system. These aren't just users sitting at keyboards; they can be people, other systems, or even hardware components. Each actor represents a role that has specific goals when interacting with our system. In our shopping example, we might have actors like "Customer," "Warehouse System," and "Shipping Provider." It's crucial to understand that actors exist outside our system boundary, which helps us clarify responsibilities and interfaces.

With our boundary and actors defined, we can now identify and prioritize our [[Anwendungsfall|use cases]]. These represent the specific goals that actors want to achieve through our system. A use case isn't just a feature list—it's a complete interaction flow from start to finish. For instance, the "Place Order" use case would include everything from selecting products to confirming the order, not just the payment step. We prioritize these use cases based on their criticality to the system's success and business value, often working closely with stakeholders to make these decisions.

The next step is modeling these relationships in [[Use-Case-Diagramm|use case diagrams]]. These UML diagrams visually represent our actors, use cases, and their interactions. The system boundary is shown as a rectangle containing our use cases, with actors represented as stick figures outside. Relationships like "includes" and "extends" show how use cases depend on each other. This visual representation helps us spot gaps in our requirements and understand the overall structure before we dive into detailed design.

In later iterations, we follow this prioritization and work closely with the client to refine our approach. We focus on the use cases that are scheduled for the current development phase, implementing them in detail. As we work through these iterations, we might discover new requirements that need to be incorporated into our development plan. This iterative approach allows us to adapt to changing needs while maintaining a clear focus on delivering value at each step.

Think of this process like building a house. In the early phases, we establish the property lines (system boundary), identify who will live there and what services they'll need (actors), and plan the essential rooms and their connections (use cases and diagrams). Later, as we actually construct the house, we focus on building one room at a time (iteration), making adjustments based on what we learn while building, and adding new features like a garden or solar panels (new requirements) as we go along.

The beauty of this approach is that it prevents us from getting lost in endless requirements gathering while ensuring we build what's truly important first. By starting with the most critical use cases and expanding from there, we create a solid foundation that grows organically with our understanding of the system's needs. This method also gives stakeholders regular opportunities to see progress and provide feedback, reducing the risk of building something that doesn't meet their expectations.

Let me walk you through the central concepts and operations of use case analysis, which forms the backbone of requirements engineering in software development. This approach isn't just about drawing diagrams—it's about fundamentally understanding what your system needs to accomplish and who interacts with it.

At its core, use case analysis helps us identify the essential interactions between actors and the system by focusing on functional requirements from the user's perspective. Think of it as mapping out the landscape of your system's capabilities before you start building roads between cities. The central concept here is the [[Use_Case_Analyse]] itself, which systematically identifies and prioritizes the most critical [[Anwendungsfall|use cases]]—those key interactions that define what your system must do. This isn't about every possible interaction; it's about capturing the essential scenarios that deliver real value to your users.

The process begins by defining the [[Systemgrenze]], which acts as a clear boundary between what's inside your system and what's outside. This boundary is crucial because it prevents scope creep by explicitly stating what your system will and won't handle. Within this boundary, we identify [[Akteur|actors]]—these could be human users, other systems, or even hardware components—that interact with our system to achieve specific goals. Each actor represents a role that performs certain actions within the system's ecosystem.

The [[Anwendungsfall|use cases]] themselves describe these interactions in detail. For example, when we model a customer placing an order in an e-commerce system, we're not just documenting a feature—we're capturing the complete sequence of actions from product selection to payment confirmation. This includes preconditions (like being logged in), the main success scenario (selecting products, confirming order), and postconditions (order recorded in the system). Each use case tells a story about how an actor achieves a goal through the system.

What makes use case analysis particularly powerful is its ability to reveal relationships between different functionalities. The [[Use-Case-Diagram|use case diagram]] we create shows these relationships visually—whether one use case includes another (like order placement including payment processing), extends another (like adding a discount to a standard order), or generalizes an actor's role (like distinguishing between regular customers and premium customers).

The operations we perform during this analysis are systematic and purposeful. First, we gather requirements by interviewing stakeholders and observing how potential users would interact with the system. Then we model these interactions in use case diagrams, which serve as both documentation and a communication tool with stakeholders. Next comes prioritization—we determine which use cases are most critical to implement first based on business value, technical feasibility, and risk factors. This prioritization feeds directly into our development planning, especially in iterative approaches like [[Scrum]] or [[Kanban]], where we deliver working increments in each sprint.

Consider how this plays out in a real-world scenario. When developing a club management system, our use case analysis might reveal that the most critical interaction is members managing their profiles. This use case would then define the system boundary—everything inside handles member data and permissions, while external systems like email clients or payment processors exist outside our boundary. The actors in this scenario would include the club manager (a human user) and external systems like the mail client and payment service provider. The relationships between these elements become crystal clear: the club manager interacts with the system to manage members, which triggers notifications sent through the mail client and payment confirmations from the payment service.

The beauty of this approach lies in its ability to transform abstract requirements into concrete, actionable insights. By focusing on actors and their goals rather than technical implementation details, we create a shared understanding between developers, business stakeholders, and end users. This shared understanding becomes the foundation for all subsequent development activities—from architecture design to testing strategies.

However, it's important to recognize what use case analysis isn't designed to do. It won't tell you how to implement specific algorithms or data structures. It won't replace detailed technical design documents or architecture decisions. And it's more formal than approaches like [[User_Story|user stories]], which are typically shorter and less detailed. Use case analysis sits at that perfect middle ground where we capture enough detail to guide development without getting bogged down in implementation specifics.

In essence, use case analysis gives us a structured way to answer fundamental questions about our system: Who needs to interact with it? What do they need to accomplish? And how do these interactions fit together to form a cohesive whole? The answers to these questions become the blueprint for building a system that truly meets user needs while staying within defined boundaries. This systematic approach to understanding system interactions is what transforms a collection of features into a well-designed, user-centered software solution.

# Slide 198

Ladies and gentlemen, let me take you on a journey through the intricate landscape of software system design, where we must answer the fundamental question: *Which components and interfaces must we actually build?* This isn't just about drawing boxes and arrows—it's about making deliberate architectural choices that will shape your system's entire lifecycle. When we examine Folie 198, we're not merely identifying functional units; we're establishing the foundation upon which your software will stand, determining how different parts will communicate, and defining the boundaries between what lives inside your system and what exists in the external world.

At the heart of this challenge lies the [[Systemgrenze|system boundary]], which serves as the architectural dividing line between your software and everything else. This boundary isn't arbitrary—it's a deliberate demarcation that clarifies what your system is responsible for versus what belongs to external actors like users, other systems, or hardware components. Think of it as drawing a circle around your software's responsibilities: everything inside that circle is your domain, while everything outside represents the external environment that interacts with your system. This concept becomes particularly crucial when we consider [[Use_Case_Analyse|use case analysis]], because each use case must clearly indicate whether its actors reside within or beyond this boundary. For instance, in an e-commerce platform, the customer placing an order is an external actor, while the inventory management system that tracks product availability is typically an internal component. The system boundary prevents scope creep by forcing us to ask: *Does this functionality truly belong within our software, or should it be handled by an external service?*

Now, let's zoom in on the actors who interact with our system. These aren't just random users—they represent specific roles that have distinct purposes and needs. An actor could be a human user performing a task, like a customer browsing products, or it could be another system, such as a payment gateway processing transactions. The key insight here is that actors define the external entities that drive our system's behavior. When we model actors in [[Use-Case-Diagram|use case diagrams]], we're essentially creating a cast of characters who will interact with our software. Each actor brings specific requirements and expectations that must be satisfied by the system's functionality. For example, a warehouse management system might have actors like "Inventory Clerk" for manual stock updates and "Shipping API" for automated dispatch notifications. By clearly identifying these roles, we ensure that our system design accounts for all necessary interactions and doesn't overlook critical external dependencies.

Moving from the external world inward, we encounter the functional units that comprise our system—what we call [[Komponente|components]]. These are the modular building blocks that encapsulate specific functionalities and interact through well-defined interfaces. Components are the architectural manifestation of the [[Modularität|modularity]] principle, where we decompose complex systems into manageable, reusable units. Each component should have a single, well-defined responsibility, following the [[Single_Responsibility_Principle|single responsibility principle]]. For example, in an online banking system, you might have separate components for account management, transaction processing, and fraud detection. These components don't exist in isolation—they must communicate with each other to fulfill user requests. This communication happens through [[Schnittstelle|interfaces]], which define the contracts between components without specifying their internal implementation.

Here's where things get particularly interesting: the interfaces that connect our components. An interface isn't just a technical detail—it's a social contract between different parts of your system. When Component A needs to use functionality from Component B, it doesn't care *how* Component B implements that functionality; it only cares that Component B fulfills the interface contract. This [[Lose_Kopplung|lose coupling]] is what allows us to change the internal implementation of a component without affecting others that depend on it. Consider a payment processing system where the main application interacts with different payment providers through a common interface. Whether you're using PayPal, Stripe, or a legacy credit card processor, the calling code remains unchanged because all providers implement the same interface. This abstraction layer provides incredible flexibility and makes your system more maintainable and extensible.

But interfaces alone don't enable communication—they need [[Konnektor|connectors]] to bridge the gap between components. Connectors are the architectural glue that facilitates interaction between components, whether they're in the same process, on the same machine, or distributed across a network. They come in various forms: method calls within a single process, remote procedure calls across network boundaries, or message queues for asynchronous communication. The choice of connector has profound implications for your system's performance, reliability, and scalability. For instance, a synchronous HTTP connection between microservices might be simple to implement but could lead to cascading failures if one service becomes unresponsive. In contrast, an asynchronous message queue provides resilience but adds complexity to your error handling and recovery mechanisms. The connector you choose becomes part of your system's architectural DNA, influencing everything from latency to fault tolerance.

Now, let's consider the operations that flow across these interfaces. Each interface exposes a set of operations that represent the actions components can perform on each other. These operations must be carefully designed to balance completeness with simplicity—providing enough functionality to be useful while avoiding unnecessary complexity. For example, a user authentication interface might expose operations like `login()`, `logout()`, `validateToken()`, and `changePassword()`. Each operation serves a specific purpose and should have well-defined preconditions, postconditions, and error conditions. The design of these operations directly impacts how easily other components can integrate with your system and how maintainable your interfaces remain over time.

The data that flows through these interfaces is equally important. The [[Datenhaltung|data storage]] strategy you choose will influence every aspect of your system's design. Will you use a relational database for its ACID compliance and complex query capabilities? Or perhaps a document store for its flexibility with semi-structured data? Maybe an in-memory cache for performance-critical operations? Each choice has trade-offs in terms of consistency, availability, partition tolerance, and query performance. Your data storage decisions will ripple through your entire architecture, affecting how components interact, how you handle transactions, and how you scale your system under load.

Let's not forget the [[Ablauflogik|control flow]] that orchestrates these interactions. How does a user's request propagate through your system? What happens when multiple requests arrive simultaneously? How do you handle failures and retries? The control flow determines whether your system behaves predictably under normal conditions and gracefully degrades under stress. In a distributed system, this becomes particularly challenging as requests may need to span multiple services, each with its own failure modes and recovery mechanisms. Your architectural decisions around control flow will determine whether your system exhibits [[Race_Condition|race conditions]], [[Deadlock|deadlocks]], or other concurrency issues.

Finally, we must consider the [[Parallelität|parallelism]] that enables our system to handle multiple operations simultaneously. Modern processors with multiple cores, and distributed systems spanning multiple machines, give us unprecedented opportunities to improve performance through parallel execution. However, parallelism introduces its own set of challenges: thread safety, synchronization, deadlock avoidance, and resource contention. The way you structure your components and interfaces can either facilitate or hinder parallel execution. For example, stateless components that process requests independently are much easier to scale horizontally than components that maintain complex internal state.

So what's the takeaway from all this architectural complexity? The components and interfaces you choose to implement aren't just technical details—they're the foundation of your system's entire lifecycle. Each decision you make about boundaries, actors, components, interfaces, connectors, operations, data storage, control flow, and parallelism will influence how maintainable, scalable, and reliable your system becomes. The most successful systems aren't built by following rigid rules, but by making deliberate choices that align with your specific requirements and constraints. As you design your system, constantly ask yourself: *Does this architectural decision serve our users' needs? Does it enable future evolution? Does it balance complexity with simplicity?*

Remember that architecture isn't a one-time activity—it's an ongoing process of refinement and adaptation. The interfaces you define today might need to evolve as your system grows and new requirements emerge. The components you design might need to be refactored or replaced as technology evolves. The boundaries you establish might shift as your understanding of the problem domain deepens. But by approaching architecture with intentionality and rigor, you'll create a system that not only meets today's requirements but can gracefully evolve to meet tomorrow's challenges.
